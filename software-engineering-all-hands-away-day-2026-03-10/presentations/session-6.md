# Session 6: Lunar Lander

_15-minute talk + 15-minute workshop_

---

## Slides

### Slide 1: Solve one small independent piece of the puzzle that is boots-on-ground right now

Session 5 was the moonshot -- the big dream. This session is the lunar lander -- the thing that actually touches the surface.

A moonshot without a lander is just an orbit. Beautiful to watch, but nothing changes on the ground.

The lunar lander is one small, independent, achievable piece of the bigger puzzle. It is something a team can start this month. It is specific enough to demonstrate, concrete enough to measure, and small enough to complete.

Boots on the ground. Real work. Real impact. Right now.

### Slide 2: Do double-diamond process

The lunar lander follows a double-diamond process:

**Diamond 1: Discovery + Design (2 weeks)**
Three amigos -- a developer, a tester, and a product person -- spend two weeks understanding the problem and designing a solution. They talk to users. They sketch prototypes. They define what "done" looks like using behaviour-driven development.

**Diamond 2: Development + Delivery (2 months)**
A pizza-team (small enough to feed with two pizzas) builds, tests, and delivers the solution. Two months. Timeboxed. Focused.

2 weeks to understand. 2 months to build. That is the cadence. Short enough to maintain urgency. Long enough to build something real.

### Slide 3: Dreic hylaw | Skillful dragon

_Empowered professional leader._

Just as the moonshot needs a champion, every lunar lander needs one too.

The dreic hylaw for a lunar lander is not a manager or a committee chair. They are an empowered professional -- someone with the skill, the context, and the authority to make decisions quickly and well.

They do not ask permission for every choice. They are trusted to lead. They report outcomes, not activities. They are accountable not to a governance board, but to the users they serve and the team they lead.

This is what empowerment looks like in practice.

### Slide 4: Top fears

- **Are we aligned enough?** -- the fear that without heavy coordination and governance, teams will diverge and duplicate effort or build the wrong thing
- **Are we agile enough?** -- the fear that our processes, culture, and tooling are not mature enough for genuine iterative delivery
- **Are we cloud enough?** -- the fear that our infrastructure, security posture, and technical capability are not ready for modern delivery patterns

---

### Slide 5: Top Levers

- **Singing from the same hymnbooks** -- alignment through shared principles, shared language, and shared understanding rather than through heavyweight governance
- **Agile principles** -- returning to the core principles of the Agile Manifesto: working software, customer collaboration, responding to change
- **Cloud sandboxes** -- giving teams safe, isolated environments to experiment, build, and deploy without fear of breaking shared systems

## Advice for event creators

### Brainstorms and ideas

- This session brings the energy of Session 5 down to earth. The transition should feel natural: "We dreamed big. Now let's land."
- The double-diamond process is well-established in design thinking. If the audience is unfamiliar, a simple diagram showing the two diamonds (diverge-converge, diverge-converge) with the timelines (2 weeks, 2 months) is very effective.
- "Three amigos" and "pizza teams" are specific, memorable terms. Define them clearly and use them consistently. Three amigos = developer + tester + product person doing discovery together. Pizza team = a delivery team small enough to feed with two pizzas (typically 4-8 people).
- Address the "are we aligned enough?" fear by showing that alignment comes from shared principles (hymnbooks), not from meetings. If ten teams are all following the same principles, they will naturally converge without needing a coordination committee.
- Cloud sandboxes are a practical, tangible lever. If NHS Wales has existing sandbox environments, reference them. If not, this is an opportunity to advocate for creating them.

### Insights

- The lunar lander metaphor is deliberately humble. It is small. It is functional. It does one thing well. In a culture that sometimes rewards grand plans over delivered results, this is a powerful reframe.
- The 2-week + 2-month cadence is opinionated and specific. That specificity is a feature, not a bug. Vague timelines produce vague results.
- "Singing from the same hymnbooks" is a culturally resonant phrase in Wales. It comes from the chapel tradition of communal singing -- everyone knows the words, everyone sings together, no one needs a conductor for every note.

## Workshop ideas

### Activity: Design Your Lander (15 minutes)

**Format:** Small groups of 4-5 people

**Step 1 (3 minutes):** Each group selects one of the moonshot ideas from Session 5 (or their own if they prefer). Now ask: "What is one small, independent piece of this moonshot that a team could start on Monday morning?"

**Step 2 (4 minutes):** Define the lunar lander using a simple template:

- **Problem:** One sentence describing the user problem
- **User:** Who specifically benefits?
- **Three amigos discovery question:** What is the one thing we most need to learn in the first 2 weeks?
- **Definition of done:** What does working software look like after 2 months?

**Step 3 (4 minutes):** Each group identifies who in the room could be the three amigos for their lander. Not assigning -- just imagining. "We'd need someone who knows X, someone who can test Y, and someone who understands the users."

**Step 4 (4 minutes):** Groups present their lunar lander to the room in 60 seconds. Facilitator captures them on the shared board.

**Facilitator tip:** Encourage specificity. "Improve patient experience" is too vague. "Build a prototype appointment reminder that sends a text message 24 hours before a GP appointment" is a lunar lander. Push groups toward the concrete.

**Alternative activity:** "Cloud sandbox wishlist." Each person writes down the one thing they wish they could experiment with if they had a safe, isolated environment with no risk of breaking anything. Collect the wishes, cluster them, and discuss which ones could realistically be provisioned. This grounds the "cloud sandboxes" lever in real team needs.
