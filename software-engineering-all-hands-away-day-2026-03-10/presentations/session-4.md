# Session 4: What Are Our Highest Multipliers?

_15-minute talk + 15-minute workshop_

---

## Slides

### Slide 1: Solutions over silos

A silo is a wall we build around our work. Sometimes it protects us. More often it isolates us.

When teams build in silos, they solve the same problems independently, duplicate effort, and create systems that cannot talk to each other. When teams build solutions together, they multiply their impact.

"Solutions over silos" means we ask: "Who else has this problem? Who else has solved it? How can we build something once and share it widely?"

The highest multiplier in software engineering is a good solution shared freely.

### Slide 2: Transparency over secrecy

Secrecy is the enemy of trust. When work is hidden, assumptions fill the gap -- and assumptions are rarely kind.

Transparency does not mean exposing everything to everyone. It means making our work visible to those who need to see it. It means open repositories, shared documentation, public roadmaps, and honest status updates.

When we are transparent about our progress, our problems, and our plans, we invite help. When we are secretive, we carry the burden alone.

Transparency is a multiplier because it turns individual effort into collective intelligence.

### Slide 3: Wellness over illness

In healthcare, we talk about shifting from treating illness to promoting wellness. The same principle applies to our software and our teams.

Reactive teams fight fires. Proactive teams prevent them. Reactive systems break and are patched. Proactive systems are monitored, maintained, and improved continuously.

"Wellness over illness" means investing in the health of our systems and our people before they break down. It means sustainable pace, technical debt management, and genuine care for each other's wellbeing.

### Slide 4: Top fears

- **Not invented here (NIH)** -- the instinct to rebuild rather than reuse, to distrust solutions from outside our team, to believe our problems are uniquely special
- **Exposure** -- fear of exposing secrets, confidential information, work in progress, or incomplete ideas before they are "ready"
- **Confusion** -- unclear how to prioritise wellness over illness in practice, or how to be agile in a world that still demands waterfall deliverables

---

### Slide 5: Top Levers

- **Solidarity** -- we all support each other, across teams, across organisations, across roles
- **Vulnerability** -- open discussion, public sharing, transparent ways of working that invite collaboration and feedback
- **Tranquility** -- calm, steady, sustainable ways of working that prioritise long-term health over short-term heroics

## Advice for event creators

### Brainstorms and ideas

- This session sits at the midpoint of the day. Energy may be dipping after lunch (if scheduled after the break). Consider opening with something interactive or energising.
- The three principles (solutions over silos, transparency over secrecy, wellness over illness) are deliberately framed as "X over Y" in the style of the Agile Manifesto. This is intentional -- draw the parallel explicitly.
- Share concrete examples of multiplier effects: a shared component library that saved 10 teams from building the same thing, an open API that enabled unexpected integrations, a transparent backlog that helped a manager understand why a request was delayed.
- Address the NIH fear directly. Many engineers take pride in building things from scratch. Reframing reuse as wisdom (not laziness) is important.
- Discuss the difference between secrecy and privacy. Patient data must be private. Work in progress should be visible. These are not contradictions.

### Insights

- "Multiplier" is a deliberate framing. The question is not "what should we do?" but "what gives us the biggest return on effort?" This shifts the conversation from activity to impact.
- Solidarity, vulnerability, and tranquility are unusual words in a software engineering context. That is the point. They signal that this is about more than code -- it is about how we treat each other.
- Tranquility is the most radical of the three levers. In a culture that often rewards urgency and heroism, advocating for calm and sustainable pace is counter-cultural. Lean into that tension.

## Workshop ideas

### Activity: The Multiplier Map (15 minutes)

**Format:** Small groups of 4-5 people

**Step 1 (4 minutes):** Each group brainstorms: "What is something your team has built or learned that could benefit other teams?" Write each item on a sticky note. Think about: shared libraries, common patterns, documentation, testing approaches, deployment scripts, design patterns, lessons learned.

**Step 2 (4 minutes):** Now brainstorm the reverse: "What is something you wish you could get from another team instead of building it yourself?" Write each item on a different colour sticky note.

**Step 3 (4 minutes):** Groups compare their two lists. Are there matches? Where one group has built something another group needs? Facilitator helps identify cross-group opportunities by reading out themes.

**Step 4 (3 minutes):** Each group writes one concrete action they could take in the next month to share something or reuse something. Share with the room.

**Facilitator tip:** This exercise often reveals surprising overlaps and duplications. Treat these discoveries as opportunities, not failures. The tone should be "look what we could do together" not "look what we've been doing wrong."

**Alternative activity:** "Transparency spectrum." Draw a line on a whiteboard from "fully private" to "fully public." Present a list of common work artefacts (source code, meeting notes, project roadmaps, performance metrics, architecture decisions, incident reports) and ask groups to place each one on the spectrum. Discuss where disagreements arise and why. This surfaces assumptions about what should and should not be visible.
