# Session 3: How Do We Get There in Cymraeg?

*15-minute talk + 15-minute workshop*

---

## Slides

### Slide 1: Deuparth gwaith yw ei ddechrau

*Starting the work is two thirds of it.*

This Welsh proverb tells us something every software engineer knows in their bones: the hardest part is beginning. The blank page, the empty repository, the first commit.

But once we start, momentum builds. The act of beginning transforms fear into action, doubt into learning, and ideas into reality.

We have already started. Every system we maintain, every service we support, every line of code we write -- we are already two thirds of the way there. The question is not whether to begin. It is how to continue with courage.

### Slide 2: Tyfid maban, ni thyf ei gadachan

*The child will grow, his clothes will not.*

Our systems must grow with the needs they serve. What fits today will not fit tomorrow. Code that works for a hundred users will not work for a million. A process that serves one team will not serve ten.

This proverb is a call to build for growth. Not over-engineering, but thoughtful design. Not premature optimisation, but awareness that the child is growing and the clothes must be remade.

Shift-left means catching the misfits earlier -- through agile discovery, rapid prototyping, and behaviour-driven development -- before the child outgrows the clothes completely.

### Slide 3: Nerth gwlad, ei gwybodaeth

*The strength of a nation is its knowledge.*

Wales is a nation of knowledge. Our strength is in our people -- their skills, their experience, their willingness to learn and share.

In software engineering, knowledge is our most valuable asset. Not the code itself, but the understanding of why the code exists, how the system works, and what the users need. When we share knowledge freely, we multiply our strength. When we hoard it, we weaken ourselves.

All-hands means we all work together, across silos, with mutual purpose. The strength of NHS Wales software engineering is the knowledge of every person in this room.

## Top fears

- **Blame** -- the fear of being singled out when things go wrong, which kills innovation and drives people to hide mistakes rather than learn from them
- **Leaving the middle of the road** -- the fear of taking a position, making a choice, or championing an approach that might be wrong, leading to safe mediocrity
- **Weakness** -- the fear of admitting we do not know something, that we need help, that we are struggling, which prevents us from growing

---

## Top levers

- **High-trust** -- psychological safety, blameless reflection, making mistakes faster so we learn faster, creating an environment where people feel safe to experiment and fail
- **Shift-left** -- agile discovery, rapid prototyping, behaviour-driven development, catching problems earlier when they are cheaper and easier to fix
- **All-hands** -- we all work together across silos with mutual purpose, breaking down barriers between teams, roles, and organisations

## Advice for event creators

### Brainstorms and ideas

- This session is the cultural heart of the day. Sessions 1 and 2 covered vision and strategy. This session covers how we work together -- the human side of software engineering.
- Open with the Welsh proverbs spoken aloud in Welsh, then translated. If anyone in the room speaks Welsh, invite them to read the proverbs. This grounds the session in Welsh identity and culture.
- The three proverbs form a natural arc: beginning (courage to start), growth (willingness to adapt), and knowledge (strength through sharing). Use this arc to structure the talk.
- Consider sharing a story of a blameless postmortem or retrospective that led to genuine improvement. Show what high-trust looks like in practice.
- Acknowledge that "psychological safety" can sound like corporate jargon. Make it real with examples: "It means you can say 'I broke the build' without fear. It means you can ask a question in a meeting without feeling stupid. It means you can disagree with your manager and be heard."

### Insights

- Blame culture is particularly corrosive in healthcare technology, where the stakes are high and the consequences of mistakes can feel overwhelming. Naming this fear directly is essential.
- "Leaving the middle of the road" is a uniquely powerful fear in large organisations where consensus is the default. Encourage people to take thoughtful positions, not reckless ones.
- Shift-left is a technical concept with deep cultural implications. It requires trust (to show unfinished work), courage (to get feedback early), and humility (to change direction based on what you learn).

## Workshop ideas

### Activity: Blameless Brave Space (15 minutes)

**Format:** Small groups of 4-5 people

**Step 1 (3 minutes):** Set the ground rules explicitly: "This is a brave space. What is shared here stays here. We listen without judgement." Then ask each person to silently reflect on one time they were afraid to speak up at work -- afraid to admit a mistake, ask a question, or challenge a decision.

**Step 2 (5 minutes):** Go around the group. Each person shares one sentence about what held them back. Not the details of the incident, just the feeling: "I was afraid of looking incompetent." "I was afraid of being blamed." "I was afraid of slowing the team down." The group listens without responding.

**Step 3 (4 minutes):** Groups discuss: "What is one thing we could change about how we work together that would make it easier to speak up?" Write the answer on a sticky note.

**Step 4 (3 minutes):** Groups share their sticky notes with the room. Facilitator clusters them into themes and highlights the most common suggestions.

**Facilitator tip:** This exercise requires genuine vulnerability. The facilitator should go first with their own example to set the tone. Keep it light but honest. If the group is not ready for this depth, pivot to the alternative activity.

**Alternative activity:** "Shift-left simulation." Present a brief scenario: "A new feature has been requested. The deadline is in 8 weeks." Groups must decide how to split their time between discovery (understanding the problem), design (prototyping solutions), development (building), and delivery (testing and releasing). Discuss how shifting effort earlier changes outcomes. Compare group allocations and discuss the trade-offs.
