# Session 5: Moonshot Mission

*15-minute talk + 15-minute workshop*

---

## Slides

### Slide 1: Solve a massive problem by leveraging science, technology, innovation

A moonshot is not an incremental improvement. It is a leap.

What if we could eliminate medication errors through intelligent prescribing systems? What if we could predict disease outbreaks across Wales before they happen? What if every patient had a seamless digital health record that followed them wherever they went?

These are moonshot problems. They are hard. They require science, technology, and innovation working together. And they are exactly the kind of problems that software engineers exist to solve.

We are not here to maintain the status quo. We are here to change what is possible.

### Slide 2: High-sight high-risk multi-year trailblazer that furthers high-line TOM and OKRs

A moonshot has three characteristics:

**High-sight** -- it sees further than the immediate horizon. It is aligned with our Target Operating Model and our most ambitious OKRs. It serves the vision we described in Session 1.

**High-risk** -- it accepts that failure is possible and even likely on the first attempt. This is not recklessness; it is the courage to try something genuinely new.

**Multi-year** -- it requires sustained commitment. Not a sprint, but a marathon of sprints. A trailblazer that creates a path others will follow.

A moonshot is not a side project. It is a strategic investment in the future.

### Slide 3: Dreic hylaw | Skillful dragon

*Empowered professional, NOT committees.*

Every moonshot needs a champion. Not a committee, not a steering group, not a governance board -- a person.

"Dreic hylaw" -- a skillful dragon. Someone with the expertise to understand the problem, the courage to propose a bold solution, and the empowerment to lead.

Committees diffuse responsibility. Empowered professionals concentrate it. When one person owns the vision and is trusted to pursue it, moonshots become possible. When responsibility is shared across twelve people and four governance layers, moonshots die in their cradle.

## Top fears

- **Danger to patients** -- the fear that innovation in healthcare technology could cause harm, that moving fast could mean breaking things that must never break
- **Resistance to innovation** -- the fear that the organisation will not support bold ideas, that bureaucracy will smother ambition, that "we've always done it this way" will prevail
- **Scarcity of resources** -- the fear that there is not enough time, money, or people to pursue anything beyond the immediate backlog

---

## Top levers

- **Covision** -- we see it together, we share the vision, we align around a common picture of what the moonshot achieves
- **Coaction** -- we build it together, combining skills across disciplines to create something none of us could build alone
- **Coalescing** -- we live it together, embedding the moonshot into our daily work until it becomes the new normal

## Advice for event creators

### Brainstorms and ideas

- This session marks a shift from "how we work" to "what we build." The energy should rise. This is the aspirational peak of the day.
- Open with a brief history of actual moonshots: the Apollo programme, the Human Genome Project, the COVID-19 vaccine development. Each combined science, technology, and massive ambition under severe constraints. NHS Wales faces similar constraints and similar opportunities.
- The "dreic hylaw" concept is powerful and intentionally provocative. Many people in the NHS have experienced death-by-committee. Naming the alternative -- an empowered professional leader, a skillful dragon -- gives people permission to imagine a different way.
- Be honest about patient safety. The fear of harm is legitimate and must be honoured. Frame it as: "Patient safety is not the opposite of innovation. It is the constraint that makes our innovation meaningful. Building safely is harder than building fast, and that is what makes us professionals."
- Consider asking the audience: "If you had unlimited resources and guaranteed safety, what would you build?" Then work backwards to what is actually possible with courage and creativity.

### Insights

- "Covision, coaction, coalescing" is a progression: see it, build it, live it. This is the lifecycle of any transformative initiative.
- The "scarcity of resources" fear is real but can also be a shield against ambition. Challenge it gently: "We may not have unlimited resources, but we have each other, and that is more than most."
- The biggest moonshots in history were achieved under severe resource constraints. Apollo was built with computers less powerful than a modern calculator. Constraints breed creativity.

## Workshop ideas

### Activity: Dream the Moonshot (15 minutes)

**Format:** Small groups of 4-5 people

**Step 1 (5 minutes):** Each group answers: "What is the single biggest problem facing NHS Wales that software could solve if we were bold enough?" Think big. Think about the 3 million people of Wales. Write the problem statement in one sentence.

**Step 2 (5 minutes):** For that problem, sketch a moonshot in three bullet points:
- **Covision:** What does success look like in 2030?
- **Coaction:** What skills and disciplines need to come together?
- **Coalescing:** What changes in daily work when this moonshot succeeds?

**Step 3 (5 minutes):** Each group pitches their moonshot to the room in 60 seconds. The room votes (by show of hands or live poll) on which moonshot most excites them.

**Facilitator tip:** The 60-second constraint is important. It forces clarity and energy. Encourage groups to be bold -- there are no wrong answers in a moonshot exercise. Celebrate the ambition regardless of feasibility. The point is to expand what people believe is possible.

**Alternative activity:** "The skillful dragon." Ask each person to privately write down: "What moonshot would I champion if I were given permission and resources?" Then ask: "What is actually stopping you from proposing it?" Discuss the gap between ambition and action. Identify one concrete step each person could take to move their idea forward.
